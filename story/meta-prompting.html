The problem
The invisible prompt
Every time you give an AI a direct instruction, there is an invisible step between your words and the output. "Analyze this contract" produces analysis — but the criteria, the focus, the depth, the format were all decided somewhere you can't see.
You only see the result. You never see why.
Three approaches to the same task
A · Direct
"Fix the bug."
"Build me a nice kitchen."
Model executes immediately. You see the output. You don't see why it chose that approach over alternatives.
B · Plan
"Plan how to fix it."
"Show me three kitchen layouts."
Model presents options. You pick one. You controlled the direction — but the model chose which options to show.
C · Meta-prompt
"Write a prompt to fix it."
"Show me the blueprint first."
Model produces a detailed reasoning framework. You read it. You find the biases. You adjust. Then: "Execute."

Our question
Why does "write me a prompt"
beat "just do it"?
"Give me a prompt to analyze [topic]" then "execute prompt" works way better than "analyze this topic" — why is that?
Asked directly to Cat Wu, Product Lead and co-creator of Claude Code
"My best guess is that it's changing the model from doing fast thinking to slow thinking."
Cat Wu · Product Lead and co-creator of Claude Code
"Like if you ask a coworker 'can you take a quick look at X?' — they might do an 80% pass. Whereas if you say 'this is why it's important' — they do more thorough work."
Cat Wu · Hackathon AMA, Feb 2026
"Asking the model to improve the prompt causes the model to believe that this is a more important thing to do, that it should be more comprehensive in."
Cat Wu · "We've shipped things like prompt improver" for this reason
Our hypothesis
The model reflects on the task,
not on what you want
The two-step approach forces the model to reason about reasoning — what makes good analysis, what biases to avoid, what structure to use — before doing any actual work. It's chain-of-thought at the meta level.
We tested this with 30 Opus 4.6 agents and 8 real cases from the build.

Case 2 · the recursive failure
The concept gap detector
had a concept gap
The pre-scan prompt — the one that decides which clauses get analyzed — opened with: "clauses that a consumer should worry about." The word "worry" is exactly the kind of concept word the Prewash Method says to avoid.
Fixed: "clauses where rights, obligations, or financial exposure are asymmetric" — structural patterns, not subjective judgment

Opus 4.6 · thread 1 of 5
Document Archaeology
Each section classified as boilerplate vs. custom-drafted. Custom clauses reveal the drafter's real priorities — what they cared enough to modify. Profiles the drafter type.
"This lease pattern is typical of high-volume property management optimizing for automated enforcement"
Opus 4.6 · thread 2 of 5
What Could Happen
Month-by-month worst-case scenario using the document's actual fees, deadlines, and compounding rules. Not hypothetical — built from the specific terms you're about to sign. Total exposure at the end.
Picks the most likely trigger, not the most dramatic · self-check: total = sum of all fees
Opus 4.6 · thread 3 of 5
Walk-Away Number
Maximum financial exposure as a hero dollar figure with a line-by-line breakdown. Every fee, penalty, deposit, and compounded obligation traced to its source clause. Compared to industry norms.
Forensic arithmetic: "The TOTAL must equal the sum of the breakdown items — verify your math"
Opus 4.6 · thread 4 of 5
Hidden Combinations
Cross-clause compound traps that are invisible when reading linearly. Clause 4 says one thing, clause 11 says another — together they create a trap no single reading finds.
Clause interaction map · compound risk scoring · "read these together" groupings
Opus 4.6 · thread 5 of 5
Negotiation Playbook
What to push on (they'll bend), what to mention (builds credibility), what to skip (non-negotiable). Theory of mind about the drafter's priorities. Includes a ready-to-send message.
Opus models the drafter's incentives to predict concessions · "they'll give you X if you accept Y"
